# How To Study In New Zealand As An International Student
28 Jan 2026
![How To Study In New Zealand As An International Student](https://cms.ptemagic.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/Anh-dai-dien-4.webp)

Pursuing education in New Zealand is an opportunity of a lifetime for international students. The country offers a unique mix of quality education and access to exciting student experiences combined with outstanding natural beauty. The best universities in addition to work opportunities and diversity of society create vibrant learning environments.

Depending on whether you are attracted to New Zealand through their education standards, job opportunities, or a desire to study in new and varied landscapes, there is something for every potential student in New Zealand. In this guide, we provide you with everything you may want or need to know, from course selections and visa application forms to living and thriving in New Zealand.

## Why Study in New Zealand?

![Why Study in New Zealand_](https://cms.ptemagic.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/Why-Study-in-New-Zealand_-1024x536.webp)

Choosing where you’d like to continue your international education is a significant decision, and New Zealand has swiftly become one of the top choices globally. It has many exciting attractions, ranging from its respected qualifications to its stunning landscapes and warm culture, which make it an ideal destination to base your educational adventure.

New Zealand is recognized as a leading study destination for international students, known for its high-quality education, diverse course offerings, and excellent post-study work opportunities.

### World-Class Education System

The quality of education is one of the factors that draw the most students from around the globe to New Zealand. The universities in New Zealand are ranked high in the global education structure because the country provides academic rigour along with practical knowledge.

The education system in New Zealand focuses on innovation, research, and practical knowledge, so your qualification will be recognized worldwide as well as in New Zealand.

What is even more remarkable is that New Zealand qualifications are recognized globally, and this is done to ensure that you are employable and have skills which are of use in today’s world.

International students rate New Zealand highly for its education quality, safety, and student support, making it a top choice for those seeking a rewarding study abroad experience.

### Quality of Life and Safety

It is always ranked as one of the safest and peaceful nations globally and ranks extremely high on the criteria for peaceful nations and the quality of life afforded thereto. This translates into a safe environment for students with minimal crime rates and extremely stable political structures that prioritize the welfare of students. As an international student, you can expect a high level of safety and an excellent quality of life while studying in New Zealand.

Students in New Zealand can look forward to good access to public services, good health care, and a lifestyle which promotes educational achievement as well as personal development. A laid-back lifestyle with emphasis placed upon maintaining a balanced lifestyle between work and play ensures that you can enjoy yourself outside the classroom just as much as you can inside.

### Work and Career Opportunities

It’s not just a matter of getting a degree when one studies in New Zealand. It’s also a chance to get work experience. International students are allowed to work part-time when they are in semesters and full-time when they are on holidays. This is a good way for them to contribute towards funding their lifestyle. However, the rules regarding the number of work hours for student visas have changed and are more advantageous for international students. Students enrolled in a PhD or master by research program are not restricted in their working hours and can work unlimited hours during their studies.

After graduation, students have an opportunity to apply for a post-study visa that would enable them to spend time in New Zealand and acquire valuable experience before building their careers.

### Cultural Diversity and Student Experience

New Zealand is a multicultural country, where people from across the world reside, learn, and work together. Major cities such as Auckland are home to hundreds of different cultures, ensuring global students feel like they are among their own people but are also able to experience something different.

In addition to this, Māori culture is an enriching factor for national identity, with Māori culture and language adding an extra dimension to learning.

The universities also have excellent support structures – right from orientation programmes to services – to help international students ease into their environment and make the most of their experience abroad.

### Natural Landscape and Lifestyle

One of the most memorable aspects of studying abroad in New Zealand has got to be its amazing natural backdrop. New Zealand’s geography encompasses mountains topped with snow, forests, blue shores, and volcanic regions.

If your passion is hiking, skiing, kayaking, or simply exploring the outdoors with friends, you can do it all in a setting as magical as it is endless in New Zealand. The country’s affinity for nature is more than just a pretty backdrop, its impact on the student experience should not be underestimated in maintaining a balance between academics and fun.

## Overview of the New Zealand Education System

![Overview of the New Zealand Education System](https://cms.ptemagic.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/Overview-of-the-New-Zealand-Education-System-1024x536.webp)

If you’re thinking about moving to New Zealand as an international student to study there, it’s a good idea to take some time to understand how the education system works – right down to qualifications you may choose to gain and typical study patterns you may be expected to follow. To begin your studies and proceed with visa applications, you must enrol with an approved education provider in New Zealand. New Zealand’s education system is very well-organized and internationally renowned, no matter whether you choose to take a vocational or tertiary education program.

### Types of Qualifications (Certs, Diplomas, Degrees)

New Zealand’s education system offers a wide range of qualifications suited to different goals and backgrounds. These include:

- Certificates: These are entry-level credentials. They offer foundation knowledge and skills. These are often in applied subjects.
- Diplomas: These are mid-tier certifications, which move on from diplomas, typically preparing students for a certain industry or for further studies.
- Bachelor’s Degrees: Usually taking three years for completion, but professional courses like engineering or medicine take longer.
- Postgraduate Qualifications: Graduate diplomas, master’s degrees, or doctoral (PhD) programmes for advanced studies or research.

All qualification levels are aligned to the New Zealand Qualifications Framework (NZQF), which promotes a degree of consistency to ensure quality in institutions, making it simpler for companies globally and universities abroad to accept your credentials.

### Universities vs Polytechnics vs Private Providers

In New Zealand, you’ll choose between different types of education providers depending on your academic and career goals:

- Universities – These are government-funded institutions that emphasise research and academic study. They offer certificates, undergraduate and postgraduate degrees across diverse fields.
- Institutes of Technology and Polytechnics (ITPs) – These are also government-run and offer both vocational and academic education. The ITPs award certificates and diplomas and sometimes degrees, which have a more vocational emphasis.
- Private Training Establishments (PTEs) – Private providers usually provide specific courses in such fields as business, hospitality, tourism, and arts. PTEs are also recognized and accredited by the New Zealand Qualifications Authority (NZQA), although they tend to focus more on short-term courses.

Collectively, these options provide students with flexibility – you can begin with a certificate and then upgrade to higher awards if desired.

### Academic Calendar & Intakes (Feb & Jul)

In New Zealand, most study programs operate in the same academic year pattern:

Two Main Semesters

- Semester 1 : February to June
- Semester 2: Jul to Nov

The following are key intakes for undergraduate and postgraduate programmes in most universities and many ITPs.

Although February and July are considered to be the major entry points, some vocational and private institutions also provide start dates (sometimes every month or every quarter) to allow for some flexibility for students.

One tip for international applicants would be to plan the whole application process 6 months prior to the intake that you would like to join.

### English-Taught Programmes and Language Requirements

The language of tertiary education in New Zealand is mainly English. This is to say that you will be learning and conducting your transactions in English. If you are not an English-speaking national, you might need to show proof of language competence to universities using tests such as IELTS or TOEFL.

The general English language requirement varies depending on the course of study:

- Diplomas & Bachelor’s Degrees: IELTS test score around 6.0 overall, with a minimum score of 5.5
- Postgraduate Programmes: Usually higher, around IELTS 6.5 overall with a minimum of bands such as 6.0

These criteria will be institution- and course-specific, so it is important to reference the specific details with the University or college you choose to attend.

While the usual requirement is the recognized test result, universities may also take into account the educational background completed for English or even offer conditional offers to include English study before embarking on further education.

## Choosing What and Where to Study

![Choosing What and Where to Study](https://cms.ptemagic.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/Choosing-What-and-Where-to-Study-1024x536.webp)

Choosing subjects to study and where to do so is one of the most thrilling, yet also highly significant, steps you are going to take on your journey to be a student in New Zealand. It’s crucial to select the right course that matches your career aspirations and future plans, as this can impact your job prospects and potential immigration pathways. With so many different programmes on offer and excellent institutions to study at in wonderful cities, here’s the process you should follow in reaching the next stage of your journey.

### Popular Courses & Study Fields

A major factor why many international students want to study in New Zealand is its unique and diverse offerings of fields of study. The most sought after programmes of study:

- Business and Management – Business degrees, marketing, finance, and entrepreneurship are popular worldwide.
- Engineering & Technology – Civil, Mechanical, Electrical, Software, and various disciplines of engineering are in great demand, with many courses having a very strong industry interface.
- Information Technology & Computer Science – More people are seeking programs in information tech, cybersecurity, data science, and software development due to current trends in information technology.
- Health Sciences – Nursing, medicine, and health-related courses are in high demand because of their practical applications in the job market.
- Agriculture & Environmental Sciences – There are Agriculture & Environmental Sciences courses offered because New Zealand has strong expertise in sustainable agriculture and environmental stewardship.
- Hospitality & Tourism – The tourism industry in New Zealand is thriving; thus, a degree in hospitality management or tourism can be a good starting point for a career.
- Creative Arts & Design – Universities also have excellent programs for fine arts, media, and design-related disciplines.

There are literally thousands of accredited programs at all levels – from certificate through to diploma, undergraduate, and postgraduate, so you can choose to study in the ways that suit you.

### Top Universities and Cities (Auckland, Wellington, Christchurch, Dunedin)

Although it is a small country, New Zealand offers some of the world’s best universities, which draw hundreds of international students every year.

Top Universities

- University of Auckland – Ranked number one in New Zealand, this university excels in many programs in business, engineering, law, health sciences, and so much more.
- University of Otago – Based in Dunedin, the University of Otago is the oldest university in the country and prestigious in the fields of health sciences and research.
- Victoria University of Wellington – Provides excellent programmes in humanities, law, and policy studies in the national capital.
- University of Canterbury – Christchurch-based, known for engineering, science, and technology programs.
- University of Waikato, Massey University, Lincoln University & AUT – Other Institutions: Other reputable institutions spread across different cities with varying course options.

Top Student Cities

- Auckland – New Zealand’s largest, most diverse city, providing a lively student life and broad programme offerings.
- Wellington – The capital city, perfect for students of politics, arts, media, and business.
- Christchurch – Known for engineering and technology fields.
- Dunedin – A traditional university town, Dunedin has a lively student scene and historic university buildings.

Picking the right location can be very important for your overall experience. It can vary from lifestyle and living expenses to networking and culture.

### How to Shortlist Programmes

Picking the right program can literally be overwhelming, especially given the many available choices. One way to simplify the process is this:

- Set Your Goals: Consider your future career plans and if you are interested in a job-specific Skills Based Course or a more academic research-oriented degree.
- Curriculum & Outcomes: Find out what courses entail, assessment methods, specializations available (digital marketing or general business, say), and what type and amount of work placement they provide.
- Entry Requirements Comparison: Consider the academic entrance requirements, English language tests, and/or portfolio/test entrance requirements if applicable, especially within highly competitive programs.
- Career Prospects: Certain sectors such as IT, engineering, or health sciences may experience a more significant demand and may offer better career opportunities. Explore the graduate employability rates.
- Location & Lifestyle: Think about whether a big city lifestyle, like in Auckland, or a university community experience, like in Dunedin, appeals to you. Lifestyle and costs can be quite different.
- Seek advice & Use tools: You can make informed choices using the websites of universities, online open days, student counselors, or comparison sites to evaluate tuition costs, course duration, or student reviews.

### Understanding Course Levels (Undergrad, Postgrad, PhD)

The education system in New Zealand provides various levels of education, catering to different phases of educational and professional development, namely:

Undergraduate (Bachelor)

- Typically 3 years full-time.
- Gives you essential knowledge and skills in your field of choice.
- Preferred if commencing tertiary education immediately after completing secondary education.

Postgraduate (Master’s & Graduate Diplomas)

- Postgraduate certificates, diplomas, and Master’s degrees normally take 1 – 2 years.
- A masters program can be taught or research based.
- Excellent choice for specializing further in your area of expertise.

PhD (Doctoral)

- Involves original research and usually requires 3-4 years for completion.
- Candidates pursuing a PhD collaborate with their advisors and could be contributing to genuine scholarship or scientific breakthroughs.

Each level will have criteria for entry, for instance, a bachelor’s would demand entry with secondary school qualifications, while for entry onto a master’s, one would need an undergraduate degree. For PhD, one needs a master’s with a substantial research element.

## Entry Requirements

Prior to commencing your research in New Zealand, all foreign students are required to fulfill a set of conditions of entry, which are both academics and English language-related. This satisfies a set of criteria which ensures that you are ready for the level of study that you are undertaking, whether it be graduate, postgraduate, or a PhD, and that you are also capable of fully engaging in study being conducted in English.

![Entry Requirements](https://cms.ptemagic.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/Entry-Requirements-1024x536.webp)

### Academic Qualifications Needed

For international students, admission requirements are primarily based on levels of study in relation to:

- Undergraduate Degree (Bachelor’s) – In most cases, it is expected that you have finished your secondary education on a level equivalent to university entrance requirements in New Zealand. This usually entails 12 years of formal education, along with certificates like ‘A’ Levels or International Baccalaureate Diploma.
- PostgraduateCourses(Masters, Graduate Diplomas)– A bachelor’s degree from a recognized institution is a necessity in most universities. There may also be certain subject matter requirements or academic achievement in the previous degree.
- PhD (Doctoral Studies) – Typically, one would have to pursue research for a PhD. It requires a master’s degree with research or an equivalent bachelor’s degree.

At all levels, some courses – or courses that are very competitive – may have different or extra requirements, such as a creative course that requires a portfolio or proof of professional experience.

### English Language Criteria (IELTS, PTE Academic, Others)

Since most programs in New Zealand are delivered in English, institutions require a proof of proficiency in English if it’s not your native language. These usually comprise:

- PTE Academic
- TOEFL iBT
- IELTS Academic
- Duolingo English Test
- Cambridge English Qualifications
- NZCEL (New Zealand Certificate in English Language) (as recognised by NZQA)

Typical minimum scores:

- In regard to undergraduate studies, it typically requires an overall PTE Academic score of about 50 with no score below around 42 for speaking and writing skills.
- For Postgraduate programmes, the criteria are typically tougher, with the majority demanding PTE Academic scores of 58 or above, with minimum sub-scores decided by the institution.
- For PhD students, similar postgraduate-level scores in the PTE Academic test (or other English language proficiency tests) are usually also required.

In other instances, if a candidate has previously obtained education that was done entirely in English, such as in a situation where the language of learning is English, the scores for English language tests may be waived by the institution.

### Specific Requirements by Level (Bachelor’s, Master’s, PhD)

Bachelor’s Degrees
 To enter an undergraduate programme, typically, the following documents are required:

- Finished secondary education and held appropriate credentials (e.g., high school, IB, or A-levels).
- English proficiency (as defined above).

In addition, specific universities may require minimum grades for qualification based on your previous education in your home country’s education system.

Master’s & Postgraduate Diplomas
 For Postgraduate study, you normally require:

- Relevant Bachelor’s degree with satisfactory grades.
- English language proficiency at a higher level than that required for an undergraduate degree.
- Certain programs will require additional documents such as statement of purpose (SOP) or reference letters.

PhD/Doctoral Programmes
 Entry to a doctoral program may require:

- Master’s degree with a research component or equivalent honors bachelor’s degree, dependent upon the institution.
- A research proposal highlighting your topic of interest.
- English proficiency to a level adequate for postgraduate study by research.

Certain PhD courses may also require proof of the quality of your academic writing and research abilities, or interviews with potential supervisors.

### Evidence of Previous Education

When applying, universities will require you to provide your academic transcripts and certificates from your previous schools/universities in which you have been studying. This is for them to check if your previous qualifications can be regarded as meeting the requirements for your chosen programme.

- Secondary school transcripts (for Bachelor’s entry)
- University transcripts and degrees (for postgraduate or PhD)
- Certified translations if your documents aren’t in English
- Sometimes credential evaluations by NZQA (for certain non-NZ qualifications)

It’s the responsibility of the individual to offer full and valid documentation, because incomplete documentation might cause delays in the admission and/or visa process. Finally, some universities require documentation of subject prerequisites if there are academic requirements necessary for the course of interest.

## The Application Process

Once you’ve chosen your course and institution, the next step is navigating the application process. Contrary to other countries, a centralized platform where one applies to institutions of higher learning is not in place in New Zealand. Here, a student applies to each institution directly. Although this is beneficial, organization is paramount.

![The Application Process](https://cms.ptemagic.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/The-Application-Process-1024x536.webp)

### How to Apply to Universities (No Central System)

In the case of New Zealand, international students apply to universities via the official website of either the university or learning provider. Each learning provider has its own admissions system.

Generally, the procedure entails:

- Choosing your preferred program through the university website
- Making an online application account
- Upload necessary documents
- Submitting the application and waiting for an offer

As there isn’t an overall system, you can apply to several universities simultaneously. This may raise the chances of getting an acceptance letter. Few universities do not charge an application fee, and this fee is waived for international students too, but it may vary.

### Required Documents Checklist

Although slight variations may exist from one institution or level of courses, nearly all applications will require you to submit the following documents:

- Academic transcripts and certificates of previous education
- English language proficiency test results (like PTE Academic, IELTS, etc.)
- Passport Copy (Photo Page)
- Statement of Purpose or Personal Statement (especially for postgraduate study)
- Curriculum Vitae (CV), typically for postgraduate or professional courses
- Letters of Recommendation or Academic References (usually required for master’s or PhD programs)
- Research proposal: PhD and research programmes

Docs must all be complete, clear, and translated to English if they were originally in another language. Accuracy in documentation is very important because inaccuracies can lead to receiving your application late or even confusion regarding your visa application.

### Application Deadlines & Intakes

Institutions in New Zealand offer two types of admission in a year:

- February intake (Semester 1)
- July intake (Semester 2)

It is generally advised to apply at least 4 to 6 months prior to the start date to facilitate the processing of offer letters, acceptance, and applying for student visas.

Some courses, especially in vocational studies, private colleges, or English language courses, might have extra intakes in a year, giving international students more flexibility. Those for competitive programmes or those with limited seats usually have an earlier deadline; hence, it’s always important to confirm the programme-specific deadlines.

### Tips for Acceptance Success

Preparation is key to increasing the likelihood of receiving an offer. To start with, you should ensure that you qualify academically to undertake the program. The next thing is to take time to compose a proper and focused statement outlining your academic interests, goals, and reasons why you want to pursue that program.

Satisfying the minimum requirements in English proficiency and academics is necessary, but having good supporting documentation can make a difference in an application. Early application can be an advantage, given that some courses consider applications on a rolling basis. If you have doubts about your eligibility, you can ask admissions departments at many universities for help before applying.

### Applying for Short Courses or English Programmes

In case you do not yet meet program or English language entry requirements for your chosen degree, there are many alternatives to choose from to help you get started in your learning journey in New Zealand.

Such courses may be provided by universities, polytechnics, or private organizations and could involve:

- Lower entry requirements
- Multiple start dates throughout the year
- Shorter application processing times

English Language Courses, Foundation Studies, or Bridging Courses may be helpful in developing the skills needed to enable the individual to progress on to a full qualification. Often, if the individual passes these courses, they may be able to progress directly on to their main course without having to take another English language test.

For short courses, or if your family members wish to accompany you for a short period, visitor visas may be an option.

## Tuition Fees & Cost of Living

![Tuition Fees & Cost of Living](https://cms.ptemagic.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/Tuition-Fees-Cost-of-Living-1024x536.webp)

It is also important for you to have an understanding of the financial realities that come with studying in New Zealand. Tuition fees and financial support options may differ for domestic students, who often pay lower fees and have access to additional funding compared to international students. This is because the financial aspect will play a crucial role when you decide to study abroad. With proper planning and decision-making, you will be able to manage the process effectively.

### Typical Tuition Fees by Level (Undergrad, Postgrad, PhD)

Tuition fees in New Zealand vary depending on the level of study and the institutions you select. On average:

- The tuition fees for an undergrad degree are normally ranging between NZD 22,000 – NZD 32,000 a year. This could be for a business or arts degree. It could also be for a science-based degree.
- Postgraduate programs might be slightly higher. The range that is often expected for master’s level post-grad degrees is usually NZD 26,000 to NZD 37,000 per year, with some other specialized degrees (like business and engineering) on the higher side.
- PhD programs are relatively lower, with many institutions charging international students a fee of NZD 6,500 to NZD 9,000 each year, similar to the domestic cost, as PhD courses are highly research-oriented.

These figures are approximate and may vary by institution, field of study, and campus. It is best to confirm prices by consulting the website of the individual universities.

### Estimated Cost of Living (Accommodation, Food, Transport, Utilities)

Living expenses take a considerable part of your budget, especially if you consider such factors as location (city) and living conditions. Immigration New Zealand will require evidence of sufficient funds (at least NZD20,000 annually) for living expenses during studies from international students.

Regular living expenses would include:

- Accommodation: The rental rates could vary significantly. Shared apartments or college accommodations could cost between NZD 600 to 1,400 in a month, and private studio apartments could be costlier, particularly in urban centers such as Auckland and Wellington.
- Food and groceries: NZD 300 – NZD 700 monthly; this depends on home cooking and going out to eat.
- Transportation: Public transportation is relatively cheap and may vary from NZD 100 to NZD 200 monthly for the average city. Discounts for students exist.
- Utilities & Internet: Budget NZD 200 to NZD 300 a month if sharing with flatmates.
- Health insurance: Since you are an international student, you require health insurance. The approximate health insurance costs NZD 500 to NZD 900 per year.

Overall, a fair estimation of the cost of living can be estimated to be within NZD 20,000 to NZD 28,000 a year, but could be higher in major urban centers.

### Budgeting Tips for Students

Living in another country involves compromise on lifestyle and financial prudence. The following tips will be helpful to stretch your budget:

- Opt for sharing an apartment: Living with other students or in university dormitories may help cut down on rentals compared to solo apartments.
- Cooking at home means that you pay less compared to eating at restaurants. Meal planning will help you save money.
- Take advantage of student discount schemes: Some transport, museums, stores, and events provide student discount schemes. Such schemes pay off in the end.
- Keep track of expenses: By making a budget form every month for rent payments, food, bills, and fun expenses, you can easily stick to your budget. Saving a certain amount every month for current expenses or future requirements can make your finances more secure.
- Part-time employment: As long as you have a valid student visa, you can work part-time during semester periods and full-time during holidays. This will help you pay off your own expenses along with giving you experience.

### Comparing Cities (Cost Variations)

Where you attend school may determine how much you pay, for example:

- Auckland and Wellington: These will usually be the most expensive for students studying in New Zealand. While the cost of the necessities such as rent and living expenses is higher, so is the opportunity for part-time employment.
- Christchurch: Relatively more budget-friendly than Auckland and Wellington. Provides the right mix of urban and rural lifestyles with relatively low living expenses.
- Regional towns such as Dunedin, Hamilton or Palmerston North: Typically have lower costs of living, including costs of rent and general living, making them very appealing to students on tight budgets.

In many respects, a smaller city would be cheaper but one must consider what kind of learning environment, social life, and career opportunities are desired.

## Scholarships & Financial Support

Attending an institution of higher learning in New Zealand may seem very costly; however, students can take advantage of scholarships and funding options that enable international students to fund their studies. It is important to note that certain scholarships and financial support are only available to New Zealand citizens or residents, so international students should carefully check eligibility requirements. Various scholarships and funding options that students can apply for range from government scholarships to scholarships and grants awarded by institutions of higher learning with varying terms and conditions.

![Scholarships & Financial Support](https://cms.ptemagic.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/Scholarships-Financial-Support-1024x536.webp)

### Government-Funded Scholarships

The New Zealand government operates major scholarship schemes intended to foster global links and help deserving candidates. The most prominent scheme is under Manaaki New Zealand Scholarships, which is administered by the Ministry of Foreign Affairs and Trade. Manaaki New Zealand Scholarships normally include all tuition fees, living allowances, return economy flights, and all other expenses. Such schemes apply to university undergraduates, postgraduate, and PhD students.

The recipients of government scholarships have to return to their country for a fixed period of time after completing their studies. This allows them to apply their knowledge to develop their own country.

There are also other public initiatives or those related to government institutions that are jointly funded by agencies or institutions, which in particular areas of study may specifically target a particular region.

### University Specific Scholarships

Most of the universities in New Zealand offer scholarships for high-quality international students, making education more accessible. These examples include:

- University of Auckland International Student Excellence Scholarship – offers up to NZD 10,000 to cover tuition fees and is merit-based.
- Victoria University of Wellington Tongarewa Scholarship – Provides NZD $5,000 to NZD $10,000 towards fees for first-year undergraduate and postgraduate-degree students.
- University of Waikato Vice-Chancellor’s International Excellence Scholarship – worth a maximum of NZD 15,000. This scholarship targets high-achieving first-year international students.
- AUT International Scholarships – these scholarships offer partial tuition fees of up to several thousand NZD.

Universities will also provide scholarships for specialized study based on programme, nationality, or academic achievement, with potentially dozens or even hundreds of scholarships offered by a single institution alone.

### Eligibility & How to Apply for Scholarships

In most scholarship programs, eligibility requirements may be based on academic achievement, language proficiency, specialized study programs, or leadership skills. In many scholarships, candidates must hold an admission offer prior to applying.

In order to apply for admission to any of these scholarship schemes, students usually need to provide their transcript and proof of offer of admission, references, an application form, and sometimes a statement of purpose. Fully funded schemes such as Manaaki scholarships require a separate application for entry, while some entrance scholarships may automatically qualify students.

As deadlines also differ, ranging from annual deadlines for some and intake deadlines for others, it is important that applicants confirm deadlines with the scholarship or university they are applying to.

### Tips to Maximise Scholarship Success

Obtaining a scholarship can be very competitive, and hence, one needs to prepare. It is recommended that you begin your search for scholarship offers early using official websites or scholarship search tools, applying only to those awards you qualify for.

A good application should demonstrate academic excellence, articulate your reasons for applying, and indicate the benefits applying for the programme would bring to your future or present community. Being well-prepared for any kind of interview or meeting the deadline to turn in your documents may also boost your chance.

## New Zealand Student Visa Guide

Picking a study pathway in New Zealand means applying not just for a place at an approved education provider but also having an appropriate visa. Knowledge of visa categories, cost criteria, documents, health tests, processing times, as well as common mistakes, will ensure a smoother visa application process.

If you plan to bring your children or dependent children with you to New Zealand, there are specific visa options and requirements to consider. Dependent children may be eligible for student or visitor visas, depending on their age and your visa status. In some cases, children of international students can attend primary or secondary school in New Zealand as domestic students, but you must meet certain eligibility criteria and provide evidence of your relationship. Guardian visas are also available for parents accompanying young children. Be sure to review the latest immigration policies to ensure your dependent children meet all requirements for entry and study.

![New Zealand Student Visa Guide](https://cms.ptemagic.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/New-Zealand-Student-Visa-Guide-1024x536.webp)

### Types of Student Visas & When You Need Them

One visa that the international students will be applying for is the Full Fee-Paying Student Visa. This visa will enable the holder to study full-time in the country. You will require this visa if your study is for more than three months and you will be resident in the country for the purpose of studying. This visa will grant the student the necessary time to stay in the country for the whole duration of their study (four years) and can allow the student to work part-time while studying and full-time during the holidays.

There are also Pathway Student Visas for individuals who are studying a series of associated courses, in addition to English Language Student Visas for people studying English language courses. These require a similar set of documents but may require a few differences based on your course type and length.

Firstly, a visa application needs to be done before embarking on a trip to New Zealand, and the offer of a place from an approved provider will be a part of this application process.

### Proof of Funds Requirements (NZ$20,000+)

A critical component of your application for the student visa is showing that you have enough funding for living costs. Immigration New Zealand typically asks for proof that you have access to funds of at least NZ$20,000 per year for living expenses when you are going for one year or more of study. For courses that are of a shorter duration, the amount will be proportionate, approximately NZ$1,667 each month.

This fund will also be distinct from the tuition fees you are required to pay and which you must demonstrate you can pay either through documentation of payment receipts, proof of scholarships, or proof of funding.

The acceptable proof of funds should include statements from banks, fixed-term deposits, education loans, and/or sponsor financials. All such proofs should reflect that the money is coming from an authentic and traceable source.

### Required Documents for Visa Application

You would require the following documents to apply for a New Zealand student visa:

- Valid passport with at least three months’ validity beyond intended stay.
- An offer of place from an approved education provider which includes information on a course and dates.
- Evidence of funds to cover living expenses and tuition fees.
- Proof of health and character (such as medical certificates or chest X-rays when required).
- If applicable, police certificates for character checks, particularly for longer stays.

Your provider or immigration advice services can help you prepare and collate these documents. It is essential that all of the information is crystal clear, accurate, and translated into English where required.

### Health/Medical and Insurance Requirements

New Zealand also requires all applicants for a student visa to be of good health. Applicants are generally asked for health information and, in most instances, are asked for chest X-rays and/or medical certificates, particularly if they intend to stay in New Zealand for a period exceeding six months.

Apart from fulfilling health requirements, you will also need to secure extensive medical insurance coverage throughout your stay. Medical coverage should provide medical care, hospitalization, evacuation, and transportation. This requirement is a visa requirement, and your visa application will be rejected if you do not have this coverage.

### Visa Processing Times & Fees

The processing of visas takes different times based on the country in which you apply for the visa, in addition to the completeness of your application. Online applications for students take about 25-45 days on average, though this can take longer during holiday periods.

The cost of the application fee may change, but for an online student visa, it should be around NZD 850 (for a full fee-paying student visa), but you must check the Immigration New Zealand site for the best information when you apply.

Applying well in advance, or ideally three months before your program, is highly recommended. This prevents delays and ensures you obtain your visa on time before you can pursue your education.

### Common Visa Mistakes to Avoid

Often, the applications for visas for studying purposes get delayed or rejected due to avoidable errors. Missing or inadequate financial documentation is one such reason. This would include a bank statement that fails to prove adequate financial resources

Other common mistakes include the failure to submit obligatory health or character documents, such as police certificates or medical statements when required, as well as the presentation of expired passports or identity documents.

Secondly, many individuals wait for too long before applying for the visa. This may result in the application taking longer and the individual not receiving the visa before the time for the commencement of their course. This could have been avoided by planning and making necessary preparations.

## Working While Studying

Large numbers of international students opt to work in New Zealand while studying. This is mostly done in order to earn additional funds that help cover living expenses. Nonetheless, it is important to understand the terms of working entitlement with regard to the student visa and some conditions of working in New Zealand.

![Working While Studying](https://cms.ptemagic.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/Working-While-Studying-1024x536.webp)

### Work Rights for International Students (Hours & Conditions)

If you are an international student who holds a valid student visa, you might be eligible to work part-time during the period when your course is in session and full-time when your course is in recess. The right to work will depend on your visa conditions, which will be stated on either your eVisa/visa letter. International tertiary students studying full time towards a qualification in New Zealand at Level 4 and above will generally be able to work while in New Zealand.

Research students – those seeking research-based higher degrees like PhD and master’s programs – can work for unlimited hours as long as they stay full-time students too

### Updated Work Hour Rules (e.g., 25 hours/week)

As from 3 November 2025, the maximum number of hours that eligible international students can work while in study in New Zealand was increased from 20 to 25 hours weekly. This is for new visas issued from 3 November 2025 and involves international students in the secondary and tertiary levels.

If you still have the old 20 hour limit on your current visa, you are able to apply for variation of conditions or a new visa to finalise work entitlements. Full-time work is still permitted during scheduled breaks such as semester holidays and the Christmas/New Year period.

Such developments form part of greater moves to make New Zealand a desirable destination for study and help international students affected by rising tuition fees.

### On-Campus vs Off-Campus Work

Once you have a visa to enable you to work, you can either work on or off-campus provided you follow the stipulated hours. On-campus jobs could involve activities related to student services, libraries, or research labs, while off-campus jobs involve many other activities in retail, hospitality, or administration, among others.

Ensure that your visa allows work before accepting a job. Be aware that if your study program doesn’t provide automatic work authorization (such as for micro-credentials or short courses), there may be requirements for a fresh visa scope.

### Tips For Balancing Work & Study

Balancing part‑time work with academic commitments can be challenging. There are a few things you can consider to manage these situations effectively:

- Prioritize your studies: Always remember that your main reason for moving to New Zealand could be study. Organize your workload around your lectures, practical sessions, and assignment submissions.
- Plan your time effectively: If you are allocated a maximum of 25 hours a week, space your study sessions for assignments evenly rather than bunching them close to assessment dates.
- Take advantage of school services: Career services can provide assistance with identifying possible employment opportunities that suit your needs for a balance between study and work.
- Review your commitments: If your academic load picks up (such as when exams are nearing), consider scaling back your work schedule.

Many institutions and colleges provide assistance for time management through the services of a student advisor.

### Tax, Payslips & Legal Work Requirements

Working in New Zealand involves being governed by the tax and employment legislations of New Zealand. Before starting to work, you would have to apply for an IRD number (Inland Revenue Department number). It is a tax number unique to each individual that the employer uses to compute the tax to be deducted; otherwise, the tax would be deducted at the highest rate.

The employer will deduct the income tax payable on your behalf using the PAYE system. The employer must give you a payslip detailing the amount you are paid and the amount of income tax deducted. It is a good idea to keep your payslips handy and make sense of your tax code if you are getting more than one income or if your income level qualifies you for tax adjustments. This is also relevant in budgeting for your studies outside the country.

It is also important that you are employed under an appropriate legal contract of employment. This will cover your rights, terms of payment, work and hours, and that you are protected under New Zealand employment laws.

## Accommodation Options in New Zealand

Choosing where to live is a key consideration when organizing your study abroad experience in New Zealand. Your accommodation options will have a direct effect on your level of comfort, your budget, and your ability to adjust to life as a student. There may be several options to choose from, depending on whether you opt to live on campus or choose a different option.

![Accommodation Options in New Zealand](https://cms.ptemagic.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/Accommodation-Options-in-New-Zealand-1024x536.webp)

### On-Campus Halls & Dorms

Many universities across New Zealand have on-campus resident halls or villages built for students. These can range from dormitory-style housing to more modern apartment-style housing. The housing can either remain catered (meal provisions available) or self-catered (you prepare your meals). Many universities find on-campus housing is ideal for international students during their first year. This housing can offer an established community for you to connect with. Additionally, you can easily access your university classes.

Prices differ depending on whether boarding is in a hostel or dormitory, but typically, it ranges on a weekly basis from NZ$200 to NZ$400+, and catered rooms are on the higher side of this pricing structure. Early applications are necessary since places, particularly for international students, are in high demand.

### Private Rentals & Homestays

Private rentals, also known as “flatting” in New Zealand, are also a very popular option, especially for students in their second year or later who may prefer a bit more independence. You can share a house or flat with other students or live in an apartment of your own. The average weekly cost of sharing flats can vary from about NZ$120 to NZ$300+ in different parts of the country, whereas private one-bedroom apartments will cost you about NZ$300 to NZ$500+ per week. Other expenses like electricity, internet, or cable may also have to be separately considered.

There is also the option of homestays, especially if you are a new international student or would like a facilitated move into life in New Zealand. With a homestay, you stay with a host family and share meals with them as well as the home. However, the cost of a homestay can start from NZ$250 to NZ$400 every week. You apply for a homestay a number of weeks before coming to New Zealand.

### Budgeting for Rent

Rent is one of your biggest expenses while you are a student in New Zealand, and it is likely to vary depending on your type of accommodation as well as the location where you reside. If you reside in major cities such as Auckland or Wellington, you might have to pay more rent, but you can get comparatively cheaper rates within smaller cities like Dunedin or Hamilton. The average rent for sharing a flat is NZ$120 to NZ$300+ per week, depending on your location.

Apart from the rent charges, it is a good idea to include other expenses such as utility bills: electricity, internet, water bills or possibly contents insurance, if they are not already being taken care of by your place of accommodation. This can easily contribute another NZ$30-NZ$60 a week to your expenses.

### How to Find Housing Before Arrival

Arranging for housing before you arrive in New Zealand can be a great way to give you peace of mind, although this may prove difficult, especially for students who prefer to study in popular cities. Check out the following tips:

- Apply early through the university housing website – Many universities start the housing application process well before the academic year begins, and international students have priority.
- Websites such as TradeMe Property, [Flatmates.co.nz](http://Flatmates.co.nz), and student housing websites can be used to look for flats to share once you are closer to arriving in New Zealand.
- Sign up for Facebook groups for flatmates and students. This can often be a good resource to find places to stay and meet prospective roommates before you move.
- Think about staying in a hostel, Airbnb, or short-term accommodation when you first move to New Zealand if you don’t have a place yet. You would then be able to go for physical viewings to make better-informed decisions upon arrival.

As the leasing market may be competitive and prices quite different from one area to another, starting your search early with multiple possibilities in mind will help you end up with suitable housing.

## Student Life in New Zealand

Being a student in New Zealand is more than a matter of education, it’s a way to immerse oneself in a vibrant, multicultural society, establish a lifetime network of new friends, and explore one of the world’s most stunning countries. International students soon discover that the world beyond the classroom is full of possibilities for socialization and experiences that will enrich their educational pursuits.

![Student Life in New Zealand](https://cms.ptemagic.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/Student-Life-in-New-Zealand-1024x536.webp)

### Culture & Community Experience

The culture in New Zealand incorporates a unique local culture with many different global cultures. The campuses and towns are full of people from all over the world. This diversity is valued and encouraged.

There are many universities which offer activities and events which incorporate this mix of culture, making it easier to share your own culture while embracing others. As a student, you would most likely be participating in festivals, cultural events, and social activities which help you gain more insight into life in New Zealand.

### Sports, Clubs & Societies

Being part of clubs, sport teams, and student groups is one of the best ways to make the most of your time at university. Universities across New Zealand offer extensive club offerings, such as more than 250 clubs and groups at the University of Auckland alone, spanning everything from academic and cultural pursuits through to recreational activities and more unusual hobbies and they host events and activities to enable you to meet people and indulge in interests beside academic pursuits.

Regardless of whether you are interested in theatre, engineering, debating, photography, or environmental activism, there’s a good chance that a university club exists for people with your interests. Sports teams and activities are also available in many universities.

### Travelling & Exploring the Country

One of the most enjoyable aspects of life for students in New Zealand is the opportunity to experience its amazing landscapes. Starting with the mountain ranges of the South Island and moving to the beaches and geothermal reserves of the North Island, nature becomes your playground during the weekends and holidays.

Many students also take domestic holidays to tourist spots like Queenstown for its adventurous offerings, Rotorua to see geothermal attractions, and Abel Tasman and Tongariro National Parks for hiking and nature walks. The small size of the country also means that much can be explored during short holidays from study.

Exploring a country is not all about breathtaking views, it is an experience that is all about immersing oneself in culture and engaging in adventure sports in order to share an experience with friends.

### Support Services for International Students

Life in a new country can, at times, be quite challenging, but the universities in New Zealand offer a wide range of support services that can help international students adapt. These may include pre-arrival briefings, academic support, English language support, personal and career counseling, and visa information to aid international students in adjusting to life in a new country. Universities in New Zealand may also have international advisors specifically for international students.

There may be buddy systems available on some institutions where you can be introduced to existing students for help and companionship, or there may be student hubs where you can readily connect with other people and get assistance. Such systems go a long way in ensuring you feel comfortable on your study path.

## Post-Study Options

Finishing your studies in New Zealand is an achievement, but for many, it marks the start of the journey for permanent residency. The immigration system in New Zealand provides ways for students to stay and gain employment after graduation, thus allowing them to acquire the necessary experience to lead to permanent residency.

![Post-Study Options](https://cms.ptemagic.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/Post-Study-Options-1024x536.webp)

### Post-Study Work Visas & Eligibility

Upon finishing a New Zealand qualification, you may be eligible for a Post Study Work Visa, which enables you to remain in New Zealand and work there for a period of up to three years. The length of stay is, of course, dependent on the length of your final qualification.

]To be considered, you will need a final New Zealand qualification in which you have undertaken full-time study for at least 30 weeks, usually bachelors, masters, or doctoral programs. Those undertaking non-degree courses will be considered based on whether it is on the list of acceptable awards, and the work undertaken must be related to what you studied.

In this stage, students who hold a degree of level 7 or higher are allowed to work for any employer and in any capacity, while other students who are eligible may be obligated to work within their field of study, which is indicated in their visa requirements.

### Job Search Tips in NZ After Graduation

Finding a job after school is an important part of moving your career forward and accumulating useful local experience. The job market in New Zealand is also quite competitive, which means you should be proactive:

- Use university career services and job platforms: University career services and/or commercial sites like Seek or Student Job Search can help you find matching employment opportunities for graduates.
- Tailor your CV and applications: This could include making your resume and applications more target-specific by matching your resume and application letters to the requirements in the job advertisements.
- Network and connect: Reaching out on LinkedIn or attending a networking event related to a specific industry can help you network. Holding informational interviews or sending a follow-up can be a great way to stand out.
- Gain local experience: A part-time job, internship, volunteering, or placement in your chosen sector will give you valuable local knowledge. Additionally, this will also enrich your CV.

Being persevering and making use of all available resources such as student work opportunities can enable you to transition better from academia to your profession.

### PR & Long-Term Stay Pathways

If you decide you want to live in New Zealand long-term after you graduate, there are steps you can take to assist with making a transition from a work visa to residency:

- Skilled Migrant Category (SMC) : This represents the most popular option. This selection pathway applies to a points test. Qualifying factors are age, skills, work experience, job offers, and English language ability. The main requirement for being invited to submit a residence application is to secure a skilled job in New Zealand.
- Work leading to residence visas: In addition to SMC, these residence options include Accredited Employer Work Visas (AEWV) and Work to Residence schemes, which assist foreign employees seeking to gain residence status following specified requirements after temporary work entry.

After being accepted as a resident, for instance within the Skilled Migrant Category, you can apply to be a permanent resident, which will enable you to reside in New Zealand indefinitely with your family, with the possibility of applying to become a citizen later in life.

### Networking and Career Resources

Creating a professional network and gaining access to resources can greatly improve your employability prospects in New Zealand:

- University career hubs: Universities offer career advice, resume help, interview practice, job postings, as well as employer networking activities, for preparing for the job market.
- Job search sites and professional networks: Job sites like Seek, or professional networks like LinkedIn, can assist you in looking for job opportunities.
- Government and Community Resources: The Student Job Search service offers job listings available only to students and new graduates. This fills a gap between education and employment.
- Industry networking events: Industry job fairs, workshops, or networking meetings provide you with the opportunity to network with employers and industry professionals to make yourself more visible to the industry.

## Checklist Before You Go

Even before studying abroad in New Zealand becomes a reality by receiving the acceptance letter, there is plenty of preparation that must be done and this entails all sorts of planning and organizational tasks that will make landing and acclimating to life within a foreign country an easy process. The checklist that follows has all been suggested by official pre-arrival guidelines and university recommendations.

![Checklist Before You Go](https://cms.ptemagic.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/Checklist-Before-You-Go-1024x536.webp)

### Finalising Your Travel & Arrival Plans

Ensure you have your student visa approval prior to arranging your flights, as well as a valid passport for the period of your stay, which should include extra validity. Ensure your date of arrival is scheduled to be around the date you start your education program, which allows you to have time to settle before orientation.

You should inform your family members or emergency contacts about your travel schedule and save your travel information digitally and physically. You should fill out any necessary travel declaration forms for travelers as required by the authorities in the country of New Zealand and have your offer of place and visa information available when you arrive.

### Packing & Pre-Departure Tips

A good packing plan will save you stress on arrival:

- Carry your passport, your student visa, your offer letter, insurance papers, and your academic credentials in both soft and hard copies.
- Check with your airline about your baggage allowances, and don’t forget about essentials that might include a travel adapter for New Zealand, which has Type I power sockets.
- Bring suitable clothes for the changeable climatic conditions in New Zealand, with layered clothing and rainproof gear.
- You should exchange some cash into NZD before leaving so you can spend money upon arrival.
- Prescription medication for a three-month period can be brought if required, along with a doctor’s note that helps in customs clearance and medical requirements in a new place.
- Make copies of important documents and store these in cloud storage, possibly even having passport-sized photos printed for identification purposes.

International students are also required to obtain medical and travel insurance coverage for their entire study duration in New Zealand. This is mandatory under New Zealand’s Education (Pastoral Care of Tertiary and International Learners) Code of Practice.

### Orientation & First Week Guide

Orientation activities take place just before the beginning of each term. Orientation is where you get introduced to all the amenities of your school, as well as information about life on campus, among other things. Your school will notify you about the orientation.

Your first week could include activities such as collecting your student ID card, completing the enrollment process, getting accustomed to the campus and taking advantage of the academic counseling service. Attend the orientation and networking opportunities so that you meet new people, and when available, also take advantage of the airport pickup services.

### Important Contacts & Support Services

Before setting out, write down important contacts that you will need when you arrive in New Zealand:

- Your university’s international student support office — they may be able to help with accommodation, enrollment, health and wellbeing-related queries.
- Emergency numbers in New Zealand (such as 111 for police/fire/ambulance emergencies).
- In case of medical needs or travel problems, you should contact your insurance company.

Keep a list of your support phone numbers in case of loss or fraud at your banks and credit cards, and also share important contact information with your loved ones back at home. This will ensure that you are always ready for anything.

## FAQs

### Can exchange‑only or short‑term study abroad students work while studying?

There have been some recent changes to the rules, and the good news is that New Zealand has opened its doors to allow more international students on exchange or study abroad schemes to work in the country during the time they spend studying, whether they are staying for one semester or not. These schemes have to be approved and meet the requirements for a visa.

### What types of work am I not allowed to do on a student visa?

Even if your visa allows part-time work, there are restrictions. You cannot be self-employed (i.e., own your own business), nor can you be involved in activities which are not in line with New Zealandese law, such as engaging in the provision of commercial sexual services. You must hold an employment agreement and be an employee who operates under current New Zealand employment law.

### Does studying in New Zealand guarantee a job after graduation?

Attending an institution of higher learning in New Zealand does not automatically mean that one can get a job, but many of its students who finish their courses can apply for and qualify for the Post Study Work Visa to allow them to stay and work in New Zealand for a period of 3 years.

With planning and a sense of adventure, your educational experience in New Zealand could be both intellectually fulfilling and personally transformative, opening doors to future adventures in your career and personal life.
